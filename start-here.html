The Honest Promise (Start Here)

Before you install a single thing, we set the rules of the game. This module is short on purpose: get the promise straight, get the one safety rule in your bones, and draw the org chart you'll spend the rest of the build filling in.

60 seconds: turn the home PC you already own into a self-running AI side-hustle machine. Educational — not financial advice.

0.1 What this is (a machine you own) vs what it isn't

You are not here to learn prompts. You're here to build a small organization that runs on your own PC: a paper-trading committee that argues out a thesis, a content engine that turns research into posts, and a back-office that watches its own health and fixes what it can. The course mirrors a real system, EAB, that does exactly this on consumer hardware with free local models through Ollama. No $200/month API bills, no cloud lock-in.

Here's the honest part. This is not a bot that prints money while you sleep. It won't auto-trade your brokerage account, and nobody can promise you a number. What you walk away with is the machine and the method to extend it. The skill compounds; a prompt pack doesn't.

Concretely: every module ends with a build artifact, so you leave with working pieces, not notes. By Module 8 you've assembled the whole org and you understand each part well enough to add your own.

0.2 The real story: an adjuster built this on a home PC

EAB wasn't built by a research lab. It was built by an insurance adjuster and pressure-washer who wanted out of the rat race and started wiring agents together on a normal desktop. That origin sets the design constraint the whole system lives by: runnable by a normal person. In the project that idea is shorthanded as "didn't ship 200GB" — lean by default, no giant model downloads, no exotic hardware.

That constraint is why the stack you'll build stays small: a FastAPI backend, local Ollama models like qwen2.5:14b for reasoning and llama3.2:3b for grunt work, nomic-embed-text for embeddings, and an embedded on-disk Qdrant store for memory. You can follow along on the laptop you already own.

0.3 The one rule that protects you: the human money gate

Memorize this: AI proposes; a human presses go. Your agents can research, deliberate, draft, and paper-trade all day. But anything irreversible — a live trade, a money transfer, a public post — stops at a gate that only a human hand opens.

In EAB this is written down as a binding decision (ADR-0004). The architect agent has full authority over reversible technical work, but one boundary never moves: no autonomous live trades, no real-money movement, no outward publish — a YouTube upload, an external message — without a person pressing go. It's not timidity. It's the line that keeps a self-running system from doing something you can't take back.

You'll wire this gate yourself twice in this course: the go-live trading gate in Module 5, and the publish gate in Module 6. Both follow the same shape — the machine does everything up to the irreversible step, then waits for you.

0.4 What you'll have built by the end

Picture the finished machine as an org chart. A Trading desk: a committee of agents (researcher, risk, bull, bear, portfolio manager) that argue a thesis to a decision and place paper orders. Intelligence: free public signals — like Senate and House congressional-trades disclosures — feeding the desk. A Content engine: research to script to clip to a draft post. And a Back-office — an MD that watches health, an IT dept that upgrades the system, a Security dept that red-teams it — that keeps the whole thing alive while you're away.

You won't build all of it perfectly. You'll build a real, running skeleton of each, and the mental model to grow it.

Build this

The "AI org" one-page blueprint — the org chart you fill in. It's a single page with one row per department: Trading desk, Intelligence, Content engine, Back-office (MD / IT / Security), Memory, Orchestration. Each row has four columns you complete as you go: Mission (one sentence), Already have (what's on your PC today — probably nothing yet), Build smallest-first (the tiniest version that runs), and Gate (does this department touch money or publishing? if yes, mark the human gate).

Fill in the Mission column now, before Module 1. Leave the rest blank — you'll return to this page at the end of every module and watch it turn from empty boxes into a system you actually run.

Takeaways

  • You're building a machine you own and can extend — not buying a black box or a promise of income. The asset is the architecture and the method.
  • One rule outranks all others: AI proposes, a human presses go. Every irreversible action (real money, public posts) stops at a human gate you wire yourself.
  • Lean by design. The whole org runs on consumer hardware with free local models (Ollama), so the only thing standing between you and a working machine is the build.
